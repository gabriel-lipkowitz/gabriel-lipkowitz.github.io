Questions of values -- what I truly care about -- are inevitably on my mind. Should mention first of all, I do feel I'm making substantial progress on the question---in bookstore other day, realised how far I've come in narrowing down my interests, compared with the vast array of ideas in the world! Central to the discussion is the idea of "practical" vs. "unpractical" matters. GK Chesterton ("About Beliefs"): Speaking for the scientist, about things he values "They are all the more to me because they are nothing to me." And then for himself, "The Slave was he who learned useful things; the Freeman was he who learned useless things." On one hand, I find myself agreeing with the scientist. After all, I am anything but a philistine. I am not an engineer. I don't think like, value same things as, engineers. HOWEVER, I quickly realise that this would lead me into the same belief conundrum that raised so many existential doubts about my relationship to science, that fundamentally I DON'T care about science for science's sake (e.g. molecular biology for molecular biology's sake). I don't think like, value same things as, scientists. So how to negotiate this seeming contradiction in belief systems? To recognise identity as not scientist, not engineer, but applied scientist. And what practical application of science to I care about? One that could be said to be profoundly unpractical: architecture, historical conservation. Part of the reason it is so difficult to find this identity is that it is not one that fits easily, or at all for that matter, into ready-made boxes of "careers." But not necessarily bad thing: identity is special.
