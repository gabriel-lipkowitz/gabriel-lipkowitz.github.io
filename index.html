May 31

What follows is an objective description of the life of a twenty-two year old living in London, United Kingdom, at some point in the 21st Century. X has lost all physical contact with other members of the human race, at least close contact. His only interactions with friends are mediated by technology (specifically two: one called a “laptop computer”, and another called a “smart-phone” though such details are irrelevant). His life’s pursuits and work is, similarly, entirely mediated by such technologies, which display information via a screen and is inputted with what is called a keyboard, tapping at which X has become adept over the years. Sometimes he uses as an intellectual aid more primitive technologies, pen and paper, and reads print books, about ideas he is very interested in (rhetoric, architecture, archaeology). X is studying computational science: how to use these technologies for legitimate scientific purposes. He recently completed a degree, from this room. X communicates with his family half-way across the world, whom he hasn’t seen in person in about eight months, by this technology as well. X does sometimes go outside; he enjoys watching birds and trees. X stays away from other humans, let alone talk to them or form relationships. To hear “human” voices, X listens to another technology, the “radio”, and at that to voices of people whose faces he knows not, nor ever will. (In fact, thanks to the advancement of algorithms governing this technology, for all his knowledge these human voices are not even from a human, but instead produced by sophisticated algorithms, called “machine learning” and “artificial intelligence.”) X stays physically fit, exercising twice a day: once in the early afternoon, before lunch, when he either goes on an hour-long run or practices dribbling and juggling the football; and once in the evening, when he engages in strength workouts indoors, while listening to contemporary music on, again, his “radio” (or listening to those aforementioned voices). It should be mentioned that almost all his indoor activities occur in the same room. He sleeps on a sofa two meters away from his desk where he works. He eats his meals on a table between these two, and cooks a whole four meters from that. To relieve himself, his bathroom is ten meters away. All his conveniences for living are taken care of, thus. X enjoys finding creative ways of maintain humanistic connections in his technologically mediated existence. He listens to classical music, reads, takes a certain degree of pleasure in cooking and his meals, and relishes in the fresh air when he does go on his walks described previously. He enjoys writing skillfully and disseminating his writing to the world (through technology) to exert power and influence. X does not necessarily miss direct human contact; he finds it more often than not aggravating. He hasn’t had any for the past two and a half months, but will start to have a bit more soon.

On statues

There is a growing sentiment among the "educated" public in many Western countries these days that we are surrounded by a sort of "tyranny" of the past, which takes the form of statues in public places. Most often, these tyrants are statues of individuals who'd engaged in the slave trade, in one sort or another, in their time. It's really quite remarkable the anger these bronze pieces of artwork provoke in these people---protests sweeping countries, and the like. What is perhaps rather odd, though, is that while these protestors are exceedingly good at voicing their anger--throwing statues into rivers, painting them, etc.--they are far less skilled at actually articulating what provokes their anger in the first place. They go on and on about one point over and over again: that "systematic racism" still exists in our society today, and that these statues constitute "symbols" of this oppression that existed in the past. We cannot "stand" for the "values" that these men "stood" for, they proclaim, and derive great pleasure from tearing them down. But what do they actually achieve, I ask, in performing these collective acts of brutal catharsis? What does tearing the statue down accomplish for the minorities for whom they purport to "stand up" for? If anything, I would argue that by tearing down these statues they do nothing but hurt the cause of oppressed minorities in their respective countries. For they have a completely backwards idea of what statues themselves communicate. It is not the bronze statue in Holland Park that sustains systematic racism in London; it is the racist shopkeeper. It is not the Confederate statue in Charlottesville that is racist; it is the city government. Of course, these protestors repetitively make the obvious point that these statues are "symbols." But that is exactly the point; they are nothing BUT symbols. They are nothing but remnants of the past, frozen and powerless to achieve anything in the present.
