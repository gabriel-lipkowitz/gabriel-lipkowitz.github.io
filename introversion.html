The more I get to know people, and how people behave, the more I realise that in every aspect of life, and every line of work, the divide between introverts and extroverts is truly a fundamental one. This is not an unrecognised division; to the contrary, it is one that is much publicised, e.g. in such personality tests as Myers-Briggs. And I do think this division is important; while it is possible, as with every classification system, to find faults in the classification (here, people will claim that there are grey areas between introversion and extroversion, that at certain times the same person can be an introvert and in other situations an extrovert, etc.). Nonetheless, I don't think these criticisms undermine the fundamental way these personality traits do dictate how we behave with others. The introvert shies away from conflict; the extrovert relishes it. The introvert is uncomfortable in crowds, the extrovert sees in them an opportunity to influence. The introvert needs time to himself; the extrovert sees this as wasteful time. The introvert is more prone to thinking over a situation; the extrovert wants to take action. Perhaps when we are malleable, as young children, before forming identities of our own, we can truly be both; however, at least in my experience, by our early twenties certain people have become decidedly extroverted while others have become decidedly introverted. I doubt this will come as a surprise to many, since I am quite sure that most people have more or less a good idea of which category they fall into. What I wish to discuss here, which may, I hope, be a bit more novel, are the dangers of introversion. I flatter myself to think that I might indeed have a small novel thing to say because, most often, it is the case that the dangers of extroversion are today highlighted. These are the dangers of the man -- and I say man because it is indeed typically men -- who does not think before saying or acting (The Trump phenomenon). On the other hand, it is often the introvert who is pitied, who is seen as the down-trodden. The poor one who can't enjoy a party; the unfortunate one who has less influence in society; the one who thinks more but is listened to less; the bullied kid in the playground. Indeed, in my own case -- and if it hasn't already become clear, I belong to the introverted category, into which most who write at all likely belong anyways -- I have caught myself pitying myself and my fellow introverts in just this manner. Because there are often situations where we introverts look on the behaviour of our extroverted counterparts with great envy. This is how the resentment usually broods (and I should note that brooding resentment is itself a sort of characteristic of introverts, since we are less willing to voice our frustrations openly, of course): an extrovert makes a blustery remark, loudly states an opinion, etc. The introvert is set aback, knowing on the one hand that the remark or opinion is indeed ridiculous, but simultaneously knowing that he would never himself have the confidence to make a statement, or even a statement to disprove the extroverts' bluster. And so the world goes round, with extroverts driving things and introverts too meek to correct them. Such is the not uncommon view of things, or if not uncommon, then at least not terribly surprising to the reader. What is less well-recognised, I hazard to guess, is the danger of the introverts. What could possibly be the danger of the introverts, of those who either think harder about an issue -- and are thus less prone to buffoonery as their extroverted counterparts -- or are bullied -- and are therefore to be felt sorry for? The danger is one of self-absorption, of self-conceit, of self-idealisation. It is less obvious, of course, as introverts express themselves less frequently. But it is present in every introvert who feels sorry for himself as somehow a "victim" of his extroverted counterparts, or of society more broadly. It is a belief that society is stupid, and that as the introvert, you are more intelligent. The introvert is too shy to express such feelings, of course -- except when he isn't. And this is of course where the danger arises. For there is no danger if a sentiment remains within purely the individual. But you see introverts occasionally express themselves to the world; after all, as those who question the validity of this distinction will point out, sometimes introverts will be extroverted. And when they do, the result is often deplorable indeed. For when the introvert expresses himself, it is often with an unbelievable snobbishness. He says what he says meekly, but he nonetheless expresses his opinion in an extraordinarily annoying, self-righteous manner. This is the origin, I suspect, of the present identity politics dilemma in which we find ourselves. After all, those who are most representative of identity politics are those who feel most "repressed" by society, and set themselves most at odds with it.
