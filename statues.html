On statues v1

There is a growing sentiment among the "educated" public in many Western countries these days that we are surrounded by a sort of "tyranny" of the past, which takes the form of statues in public places. Most often, these tyrants are statues of individuals who'd engaged in the slave trade, in one sort or another, in their time. It's really quite remarkable the anger these bronze pieces of artwork provoke in these people---protests sweeping countries, and the like. What is perhaps rather odd, though, is that while these protestors are exceedingly good at voicing their anger--throwing statues into rivers, painting them, etc.--they are far less skilled at actually articulating what provokes their anger in the first place. They go on and on about one point over and over again: that "systematic racism" still exists in our society today, and that these statues constitute "symbols" of this oppression that existed in the past. We cannot "stand" for the "values" that these men "stood" for, they proclaim, and derive great pleasure from tearing them down. But what do they actually achieve, I ask, in performing these collective acts of brutal catharsis? What does tearing the statue down accomplish for the minorities for whom they purport to "stand up" for? If anything, I would argue that by tearing down these statues they do nothing but hurt the cause of oppressed minorities in their respective countries. For they have a completely backwards idea of what statues themselves communicate. It is not the bronze statue in Holland Park that sustains systematic racism in London; it is the racist shopkeeper. It is not the Confederate statue in Charlottesville that is racist; it is the city government. Of course, these protestors repetitively make the obvious point that these statues are "symbols." But that is exactly the point; they are nothing BUT symbols. They are nothing but remnants of the past, frozen and powerless to achieve anything in the present.

On statues v2

There is a growing sentiment among the "educated" public in many Western countries these days that we are surrounded by a sort of "tyranny" of the past, which takes the form of statues in public places. They go on and on about one point over and over again: that systematic racism still exists in our society today, and that these statues constitute "symbols" of this oppression that existed in the past. They are absolutely right that we live in a society that has deep racial inequities; only a fool would argue against that. But I'll be quite honest; I'm not quite sure why these statues have much to do with those legitimate concerns. We cannot "stand" for the "values" that these men "stood" for, they proclaim, and derive great pleasure from tearing them down. But can one ask a simple question, which nonetheless seems rarely asked: what do they actually achieve, I ask, in performing these collective acts of brutal catharsis? What does tearing the statue down accomplish for the minorities for whom they purport to "stand up" for? If anything, I would argue that by tearing down these statues they do nothing for the cause of oppressed minorities in their respective countries. It is not the bronze statue in Holland Park that sustains systematic racism in London; it is the racist shopkeeper. It is not the Confederate statue in Charlottesville that is racist; it is the city government. How many African-Americans does the statue of Robert E. Lee employ, whose wages he can suppress and whose promotions he can delay? None. How many young black men can the statue of Lord Holland kneel down on while he arrests them for petty crimes? None. Of course, these protestors repetitively make the obvious point that these statues are "symbols." But that is exactly the point; they are nothing BUT symbols. They are nothing but remnants of the past, frozen and powerless to achieve anything in the present. If anything, I would venture to say that they have a completely backwards idea of what statues themselves can communicate. Let's say for a moment that the anti-statueists achieve their goal of tearing down every single statue of a slave-trader, every single statue of a slave-owner, every single statue of any person in history who ever had anything at all to do with the slave trade (which may be every statue erected before the end of the Civil War, but that's another question). If they were to achieve such a goal, what would they have left to rally their protests around? Where would they so visibly point to the crimes of the past for justification of the very true need for reform in the present? If anything, I would venture to say that this would, in this manner, actually harm their actual cause in the present. For by erasing history, they erase our ability to remember and therefore learn from it. And so, I suggest that while not actually achieving anything by attacking these statues, if anything they impede they're ability to achieve something.
